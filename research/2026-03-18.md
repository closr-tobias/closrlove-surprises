Previous research covered: Typography animations (Mar 8), Particle systems (Mar 12), Scroll-driven storytelling (Mar 13), Sound design & audio-reactive visuals (Mar 14), Device sensor interactions (Mar 17). This session explores **photo-centric effects** — how images of the couple can be transformed, revealed, and emotionally charged through CSS filters, canvas pixel manipulation, and creative compositing.

### 1. Double Exposure Blend

**Concept:** Two images — one of the partner, one of a meaningful landscape/texture (city skyline, ocean waves, starfield, forest) — blend together in a cinematic double exposure. The blend shifts over time: the portrait dominates at first, then the landscape bleeds through, then they harmonize. Text floats between the layers: "You are my whole world."

**Why it works for Closr:** Double exposure is inherently poetic — it says "you and this place/feeling are the same thing to me." For LDR couples, blending a partner's photo with their city skyline is devastatingly effective. It's visually stunning with minimal user input (just two images).

**Technical approach:**
- CSS `mix-blend-mode: screen` (light areas merge) or `multiply` (dark areas merge) on overlapping `<img>` elements
- Animate blend transition with `opacity` keyframes on each layer
- Alternative: Canvas 2D — `drawImage()` both, use `globalCompositeOperation: 'screen'` or pixel-level blending
- Animated shift: slowly `transform: scale(1.05)` + `translate` the landscape layer for a living, breathing feel
- Color grading: CSS `filter: sepia(0.3) contrast(1.1) saturate(0.8)` for cinematic warmth
- Text: `mix-blend-mode: difference` for text that inverts against the image — always readable, always striking

### 2. Pixel Dissolve / Reassemble

**Concept:** A photo of the partner starts fully pixelated (8x8 blocks, unrecognizable). The pixels slowly refine — 16x16, 32x32, 64x64 — until the full photo is revealed in crisp detail. Each resolution step is accompanied by a line of text: "At first you were a stranger..." → "Then I started to see you..." → "Now I can't unsee you." → [full photo] "And I never want to."

**Why it works for Closr:** The reveal mechanic creates anticipation — the viewer *wants* to see the photo, and the gradual clarity mirrors falling in love (seeing someone more clearly over time). The metaphor writes itself. Works beautifully for "how we met" or anniversary surprises.

**Technical approach:**
- Canvas 2D: Load image, sample average color of NxN blocks using `getImageData()`
- Draw `fillRect()` blocks at each resolution tier
- Transition between tiers: animate block sizes smoothly (lerp block dimensions over 60 frames)
- Or CSS approach: apply `filter: blur()` decreasing from `blur(20px)` to `blur(0)` with stepped keyframes + `image-rendering: pixelated` at low res
- Each tier triggers a text fade-in via `setTimeout` or scroll position
- Final reveal: slight `scale(1.02)` + `filter: brightness(1.1)` pulse to make the clear photo feel like it *snaps* into focus

### 3. Color Drain & Restore (Selective Color)

**Concept:** A couple photo starts in full vibrant color, then slowly drains to black-and-white — except for one element the sender chose (red lipstick, a blue scarf, the color of their eyes). Text: "The world fades to grey without you..." The single color element glows. Then on tap/scroll, full color floods back in: "...but you bring it all back."

**Why it works for Closr:** The Schindler's List "girl in red coat" technique applied to romance. Isolating one color is visually arresting and emotionally loaded — it says "this detail about you is what I notice." The restore moment is genuinely joyful.

**Technical approach:**
- Canvas 2D pixel manipulation: `getImageData()`, iterate pixels
- For each pixel: calculate if it's within a target hue range (±15° on HSL wheel)
- Non-target pixels: set R=G=B = `0.299*R + 0.587*G + 0.114*B` (luminance greyscale)
- Target pixels: keep original RGB, optionally boost saturation
- Animate the transition: interpolate between original and processed pixel data over time
- The "restore" is simply animating back to original image data
- Performance: process on offscreen canvas, use `requestAnimationFrame`, consider Web Workers for large images
- Simpler CSS fallback: `filter: grayscale(1)` on the image, with a `clip-path` cutout over the colored region (less flexible but much simpler)

### 4. Photo-to-Sketch-to-Photo

**Concept:** A photo of the couple renders as a pencil sketch (line drawing). It looks hand-drawn, artistic, like someone sketched them from memory. Pencil strokes animate in (similar to handwriting reveal from Mar 8 research, but for an image). Then the sketch gradually fills with watercolor-style washes of color, eventually becoming the real photo. "I drew you from memory... but reality is so much better."

**Why it works for Closr:** Combines the intimacy of hand-drawn art with the emotional punch of a real photo. The transition from sketch → photo feels like a dream becoming real. For LDR couples: "I imagine you every day, but nothing compares to the real you."

**Technical approach:**
- Edge detection: Canvas 2D — Sobel operator on greyscale image data to extract edges
- Or simpler: CSS `filter: grayscale(1) contrast(3) invert(1) brightness(1.5)` creates a passable sketch effect
- Animated stroke reveal: Convert edge-detected result to SVG paths (complex) OR render the sketch onto canvas with progressive `globalAlpha` increase from one side to the other (wipe reveal)
- Watercolor fill: Layer the original photo underneath with `opacity: 0`, animate opacity up while the sketch layer fades
- Add watercolor texture overlay (PNG with `mix-blend-mode: multiply`) for the transition period
- Slight paper grain texture background throughout: `background-image: url(paper-texture.png)`

### 5. Photo Mosaic (Many Moments, One Image)

**Concept:** A large portrait of the partner is composed of dozens of tiny photos — selfies, date photos, screenshots, everyday moments. At first you see the big picture. Pinch-to-zoom (or animated zoom) reveals the individual tiles. Each tile can be tapped to briefly enlarge. "Every little moment with you... makes up the bigger picture."

**Why it works for Closr:** This is the visual equivalent of "it's the little things." Couples who've shared lots of photos can see their relationship literally *compose* their partner. The zoom mechanic creates a "whoa" discovery moment. Even with AI-selected stock/placeholder images, the concept lands.

**Technical approach:**
- Grid of NxN thumbnail images (e.g., 20x25 = 500 tiles, each ~20x20px at overview)
- Each tile's average color must match the corresponding region of the target portrait
- Algorithm: for each grid cell, find the input photo whose average color is closest (Euclidean distance in RGB space)
- Canvas 2D: draw all tiles at low res, overlay the target portrait with `globalAlpha: 0.3` for coherence
- Zoom: CSS `transform: scale()` with `transform-origin` at tap point, or pinch gesture via touch events
- Tile tap: `position: absolute` overlay that scales the tile to ~200x200px with the full photo visible
- Simplified version: use CSS `background-size` + `background-position` to slice one large grid image

### 6. Cinemagraph / Living Photo

**Concept:** A photo that's *almost* still — but one element moves. Hair blowing in the wind. Steam rising from a coffee cup. Waves lapping at the shore. Rain falling past a window. The rest is frozen. Text appears softly: "This moment. I want to live in it forever."

**Why it works for Closr:** Cinemagraphs are mesmerizing precisely because they challenge expectations — your brain expects a photo, but it *breathes*. This creates a hypnotic, intimate feeling. Perfect for capturing a specific memory. Even without a real cinemagraph source, it can be simulated.

**Technical approach:**
- If real video clip available: mask the still portion (CSS `clip-path` or canvas masking), loop the video underneath
- Simulated approach (no video needed): Take a static photo, add animated overlays for the moving element:
  - Rain: Canvas particle system (vertical streaks with `globalAlpha: 0.4`)
  - Steam: CSS animated pseudo-elements with `filter: blur(8px)` and sinusoidal `transform` movement
  - Hair/fabric: Subtle CSS `transform: skew()` oscillation on a clipped region
  - Water: Apply `filter: url(#displacement)` SVG filter with animated `feTurbulence` to a masked region
- The displacement filter approach is the most versatile: `<feTurbulence baseFrequency="0.02" seed="1"><animate attributeName="seed" from="1" to="100" dur="3s" repeatCount="indefinite"/>`

### 7. Duotone Mood Shift

**Concept:** A couple photo cycles through duotone color treatments — each representing a mood or time of day. Midnight blue + silver (nighttime longing). Warm amber + cream (golden hour romance). Soft pink + white (tender morning). Each color shift is accompanied by a different message: what the sender thinks about at night vs. morning vs. sunset. The viewer can tap to advance or it auto-cycles.

**Why it works for Closr:** Duotone is effortlessly stylish (Spotify popularized it). The mood-color-message combination turns one photo into a multi-layered emotional experience. Simple to implement but feels premium.

**Technical approach:**
- CSS `filter: grayscale(1)` to desaturate, then overlay a gradient with `mix-blend-mode: color`
- Or more precise: Canvas `getImageData()`, convert each pixel to luminance, map luminance to a two-color gradient (dark color → light color)
- Color pairs: `['#1a1a4e', '#c0c0c0']` (midnight), `['#8B4513', '#FFECD2']` (golden), `['#E8A0BF', '#FFFFFF']` (tender)
- Transition: CSS `transition: filter 1.5s ease` or canvas crossfade between processed versions
- Text: fade out old message, pause 0.5s, fade in new message, sync with color transition
- Auto-cycle: 4-5 second intervals, with a subtle progress bar at bottom

## Summary & Recommendations for Next Blocks

**Highest impact, easiest to build:**
1. **Pixel Dissolve** — Pure canvas, no external assets needed, strong metaphor, works with any photo
2. **Duotone Mood Shift** — CSS filters only, visually striking, supports multi-message structure
3. **Color Drain & Restore** — Dramatic emotional arc, technically moderate

**Highest impact, more complex:**
4. **Double Exposure** — Needs two images but the result is gallery-worthy
5. **Living Photo (Cinemagraph)** — Simulated version is achievable; real version needs video input

**Best for "wow" factor:**
6. **Photo Mosaic** — Complex but the zoom-reveal moment is unforgettable

**Note:** All of these work best with real couple photos, which aligns with Closr's data-driven approach — the app already collects relationship context, and photo upload/selection would be a natural extension. Even without real photos, placeholder/AI-generated imagery can demonstrate the mechanics.
